Rome in Middle Ages

Fall of Western Roman Empire

476 A.D.

Romulus Augustulus, the last emperor of the Western Roman Empire, was deposed by Odoacer, a Germanic king. Nullam id commodo turpis. Mauris auctor felis et massa pharetra, ac vestibulum nunc vulputate.

Clovis

481-511

Clovis I's reign from 481-511 solidified Frankish dominance in Gaul. He conquered Roman-ruled territories through victories like the Battle of Tolbiac, converting to Catholicism in 496 to align with Gallo-Roman subjects.

Ostrogoths

492-526

Ostrogothic Kingdom's rule over Rome and Italy under King Theodoric the Great. After defeating Odoacer in 493, Theodoric made Ravenna his capital but allowed Roman institutions and culture to continue in Rome itself.

Justinian

527-565

Justinian I's reign as Byzantine Emperor from 527-565 was marked by a renewed ambition to revive the greatness of the Roman Empire. He oversaw a significant reconquest of territories around the Mediterranean, regaining parts of the former Western Empire. His generals reconquered the Vandal Kingdom in North Africa, parts of Spain, and eventually Italy after a protracted Gothic War.

Conquest of Italy

535-554

Byzantine Empire under Emperor Justinian I launched a campaign to reclaim the Italian peninsula from the Ostrogothic Kingdom. This military reconquest began in 535 when Byzantine forces landed in Sicily and advanced into southern Italy.

Lombards

568

Germanic people known as the Lombards, led by their king Alboin, invaded the Italian peninsula from the northern region of Pannonia. They swiftly overran the majority of the Byzantine-ruled territories in northern Italy. The Lombards carved out a kingdom for themselves, centered in the rich Po Valley and stretching across much of northern and central Italy.

Gregory the great

590-604

The papacy of Gregory the Great (590-604) marked an important transition for the Roman Catholic Church's temporal authority in Italy. As the Byzantine Empire's hold over Rome waned, Gregory assumed broader civil responsibilities to maintain order and protect the population. He negotiated with the Lombard tribes invading Italy and directed diplomatic and military operations in the city's defense. Gregory embraced the idea of papal supremacy, claiming authority over all Christians.

Iconoclastic Controversy

717-787

The Iconoclast Controversy erupted in 717 when Byzantine Emperor Leo III condemned the veneration of religious icons and images. Iconoclasts believed such practices violated one of the Ten Commandments prohibiting idolatry and idolatrous imagery. This sparked religious and political tumult, as iconodules defended the long-established traditions of producing and venerating icons.

Carolingian Dinasty

717-751

Carolingian dynasty steadily accumulated power and authority in the Frankish kingdoms, while the Merovingian monarchs were reduced to figurehead status. Under Charles Martel and his sons Carloman and Pepin the Short, the Carolingians served as the de facto rulers and Mayors of the Palace controlling the royal households. Though the Merovingians technically remained kings during this period, they held little real power beyond a ceremonial role.

Charlemagne

774

After decades of conflict between the Lombards and Franks, in 774 A.D. the Frankish king Charlemagne invaded the Kingdom of Lombardy at the request of Pope Adrian I. Charlemagne swiftly defeated the Lombard forces, capturing their capital of Pavia and deposing the last Lombard king Desiderius. This decisive victory allowed Charlemagne to take control of the Lombard kingdom and its territories across northern Italy. He incorporated these lands into his growing Carolingian Empire, which already encompassed modern-day France, Germany and parts of Spain and Italy.

Charlemagne

800

Charlemagne, king of the Franks, traveled to Rome where he attended Mass at St. Peter's Basilica. During the service, in a moment steeped in historical significance, Pope Leo III placed an imperial crown upon Charlemagne's head, proclaiming him as the "most serene Augustus" and bestowing upon him the title of Roman Emperor. This solemn ceremony effectively revived the Western Roman Empire which had ceased to exist three centuries earlier. Charlemagne's coronation as the first Holy Roman Emperor united under his rule vast territories across western and central Europe.

Vikings

800-1000

The 9th and 10th centuries saw a sustained period of Viking raids and invasions across coastal areas of continental Europe, including Italy. These Norse maritime warriors, known as Vikings, launched daring attacks from their Scandinavian homelands, sailing in their iconic longships. In Italy, Viking forces sacked numerous cities and monasteries, particularly along the coastal regions.

Rus' people

911-989

The Rus' - an East Slavic people who founded the medieval Russian state centered around Kiev - underwent a pivotal shift by adopting Orthodox Christianity. This religious conversion was facilitated by missionaries dispatched from the Byzantine Empire. In 988 AD, the Rus' ruler Prince Vladimir I was baptized into the Orthodox faith, followed by the mass baptism of the Kievan Rus'. Byzantine clerics educated the Rus' and translated Orthodox liturgy and texts into the Slavic language. The cultural and religious influence of Byzantium profoundly shaped the development of the Rus' civilization. The conversion solidified the position of the Rus' within the Orthodox Christian world and their political alignment towards Constantinople over western Europe.

Otto I

936-973

Otto I, also known as Otto the Great, was one of the most influential and powerful rulers of the medieval Holy Roman Empire. His reign from 936-973 marked a pivotal period in solidifying German imperial authority across central Europe. After being crowned King of Germany in 936, Otto spent years aggressively expanding royal power and subduing rebellious noble magnates. His greatest achievement was reviving the concept of a unified Roman Empire in the West when he was crowned Holy Roman Emperor by the Pope in 962.

Pope Gregory VII

-1073-1085

Pope Gregory VII's reign initiated major reforms that significantly increased papal power and authority. He prohibited the selling of church offices and enforced clerical celibacy, putting him at odds with secular rulers. Most notably, Gregory VII proclaimed the doctrine of papal supremacy, stating the pope alone could appoint and remove monarchs. This directly challenged the Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV, leading to Gregory's excommunication of the emperor in 1076. Though briefly exiled, Gregory's policies transformed the medieval papacy into a more centralized and independent institution able to confront the perceived overstep of temporal authorities into spiritual matters. His assertion of papal supremacy over rulers was revolutionary and had a lasting impact on the Church's relationship with secular powers.

Investiture Controversy

1075-1122

The Investiture Conflict was a fierce power struggle between reform-minded medieval popes and European monarchs over who held the authority to appoint high-ranking clergy like bishops. It erupted in 1075 when Pope Gregory VII banned lay investiture, denying secular rulers' traditional privilege of investiture of bishops. This directly challenged the authority of the Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV, who refused to obey and was excommunicated by the pope in 1076. War ensued between pro-imperial and pro-papal forces across the Holy Roman Empire until a compromise was reached in the Concordat of Worms in 1122. Though not fully resolving the matter, the conflict marked an important step in asserting the independence of the Church from temporal control and establishing firm papal supremacy over ecclesiastical appointments.

Alexius Comneus

1081-1118

Alexius I Comnenus' reign as Byzantine Emperor initiated a temporary revival of the beleaguered empire after taking power in 1081. He was forced to appeal to the West for mercenary aid against the invading Seljuk Turks, inadvertently helping trigger the First Crusade in 1095. Though the Crusaders provided brief assistance, their arrival also strained Byzantine resources. Domestically, Alexius enacted military, fiscal and administrative reforms to stabilize the empire. He managed to reclaim some Anatolian and Balkan territories from the Seljuks and Normans through costly campaigns. However, Alexius also established the long-ruling Comnenus dynasty whose later rulers increased tensions with Western powers. His pragmatic policies allowed Byzantium to survive major crises, albeit setting the stage for the empire's eventual decline.

Normans

1084

In the late 11th century, the Normans under Robert Guiscard and his brother Roger initiated campaigns to conquer the Lombard and Byzantine possessions of southern Italy. After years of steady gains against Byzantine forces, Guiscard dealt a decisive blow by sacking the city of Dyrrachium in 1082 after a prolonged siege. This stunning victory, coupled with Norman naval raids, forced the Byzantine emperor to cede the remaining lands around Bari and Otranto to Guiscard in 1084. With papal approval, the Normans were invested as rulers of these conquered territories, formally establishing the Norman Kingdom of Sicily based in Palermo. This eliminated the last Byzantine foothold on the Italian mainland while providing the ambitious Normans a strategic base to expand their power in the Mediterranean.

First Crusade

1095-1099

The First Crusade was launched in 1095 after Pope Urban II issued a call for Western European knights and nobles to aid the Byzantine Empire, which had appealed for help against the encroaching Seljuk Turks in Anatolia. Over the next four years, multiple armies of Crusaders from across France, Germany, and Italy converged on Constantinople before invading Asia Minor. Despite facing fierce resistance, the Crusaders captured several key Syrian cities like Antioch in 1098. Their main endeavor was the brutal siege and conquest of Jerusalem in 1099, establishing Crusader states in the Holy Land for nearly two centuries. While achieving its military aims, the First Crusade deepened the growing cultural and political rift between the East and West and ushered in an era of prolonged conflict between European Christians and Muslims over control of the Levant.

Concordance of Discordant Canons

1140

Completed around 1140 by the Italian monk Gratian, the Decretum Gratiani was a groundbreaking effort to systematically codify and reconcile the various contradictory canonical laws and decrees that had accumulated in the Catholic Church over centuries. This comprehensive text collected and organized nearly 4,000 different sources from scripture, church councils, papal letters, and theological writings. Gratian aimed to bring clarity and consistency to canon law by resolving conflicts between sources using principles of Roman law. Studied at medieval European universities, the Decretum helped develop canon law into a sophisticated legal system independent of secular laws. It was also instrumental in centralizing and bolstering papal authority. Gratian's landmark work remained an authoritative source that guided the growth of Catholic canon law until modern times.

Pope Innocent III

1198-1216

Pope Innocent III's papacy marked a high point of papal power and authority during the medieval period. As pope from 1198-1216, Innocent vigorously asserted his spiritual primacy over secular rulers across Europe. He excommunicated kings, launched crusades, approved religious orders like the Franciscans, and even intervened in imperial elections in Germany. Innocent's political maneuverings allowed him to dominate the young Frederick II as Holy Roman Emperor. His decrees from the landmark Fourth Lateran Council of 1215 codified Church doctrine on issues like transubstantiation. Through his forceful leadership, Innocent III cemented the medieval papacy's claims of supremacy over all Christian monarchs and peoples. His legacy exemplified the height of the Church's power before it began waning in subsequent centuries.

Franciscan order established

1209

In 1209, the Franciscan religious order was officially founded by the charismatic St. Francis of Assisi. Francis, a wealthy young man who took a vow of poverty, gathered a small group of followers who preached lives of humility, simplicity and service to the poor. The Franciscan order was unique in its embrace of poverty as a spiritual pathway. It received oral approval from Pope Innocent III in 1210, followed by an official papal bull of endorsement in 1223. The Franciscans grew rapidly across medieval Europe, quickly becoming one of the most prominent missionary orders known for their evangelical zeal and humanitarian work among the destitute. Their commitment to Franciscan values deeply influenced the trajectory of Western Christianity and monastic traditions. St. Francis's legacy endures through the prominent religious order he established.
